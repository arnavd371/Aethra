aethra research
the pipeline from
curiosity to contribution.

most ambitious students hit a wall that has nothing to do with talent: no mentors, no peers working on similar problems, no pathway from an interesting idea to something rigorous and lasting. aethra research is the infrastructure for closing that gap. fellowships, mentorship, publication support, preprint reviews, monthly open problems, and research essays. everything here is in development. we are building it carefully, not quickly.

research fellowships
pilot — launching [TODO: date]

semester-length research projects pairing students with mentors from universities and industry. fellows work on a defined research question within one of aethra's tracks, meet weekly with their mentor, and produce a written output — a preprint, a technical report, or a documented open-source contribution.

the fellowship is not a tutoring program. mentors guide; fellows do the work. the goal is for students to encounter what original research actually feels like: extended uncertainty, iteration, and the particular satisfaction of having genuinely added something.

research mentorship
building mentor network

mentors are matched to students or teams based on research track, specific problem area, and working style. matching is done by hand — we do not use an algorithm because the quality of the mentor-student relationship determines almost everything about whether the work succeeds.

mentors come from universities, industry, and prior aethra events. if you are a researcher, practitioner, or graduate student interested in mentoring, reach out at aethraglobal@gmail.com.

publication support
in development

help getting serious student work into real venues: arXiv preprints, student research journals, and eventually peer-reviewed conferences where the work is strong enough. this is support, not a guarantee. we will not push weak work into unsuitable venues to pad a profile. we will help strong work find the right home and get there in good shape.

preprint reviews
in development

a lightweight peer-review layer for students who have a draft and want rigorous feedback before wider submission. reviewers are mentors and advanced students who have agreed to give detailed written feedback within two weeks. not a journal. not a prize. just honest critique from people who have read the literature.

open problems

each month, aethra releases a batch of open research questions across five tracks. each problem comes with a statement, suggested reading, a difficulty note, and a way to form a team. problems are chosen because they are genuinely unsolved and approachable with undergraduate-level (or less) background. the first batch releases [TODO: date].

sample problem — mathematics
when does gradient descent find a global minimum in non-convex settings?

gradient descent on non-convex loss functions is widely used in machine learning, yet the theoretical conditions under which it converges to a global minimum (rather than a local one) remain only partially understood. characterize a family of non-convex functions for which convergence guarantees can be stated, and identify the structural properties that make such guarantees possible.

difficulty: advanced track: optimization suggested: Du et al. 2019, Kawaguchi 2016
sample problem — AI alignment
can helpfulness be formally specified in a way that is complete and verifiable?

current approaches to AI alignment rely on reward models trained on human preference data. these are approximate proxies for helpfulness, not formal specifications. investigate whether a formal definition of "helpfulness" can be constructed that is both complete (captures all cases we care about) and verifiable (can be checked mechanically), and what obstacles arise when attempting to do so.

difficulty: exploratory track: ai alignment suggested: Christiano et al. 2017, Soares & Fallenstein 2014

research essays

short essays on research methodology, mathematical foundations, and ideas worth thinking carefully about. written by the aethra team and, eventually, by fellows. two essays are in progress; they will be published here when ready.

mathematics · coming soon
why theorem proving matters: a primer on formal mathematical reasoning

most students first encounter a proof in a university lecture, without the vocabulary to understand why it works as a form of argument. this essay introduces the structure of mathematical proofs, the difference between informal and formal reasoning, and why learning to construct arguments from first principles is one of the most transferable things a student can do.

ai research · coming soon
what LLMs cannot do: reasoning limits and what they reveal

large language models are impressive at pattern completion and surface-level inference, but struggle with systematic reasoning that requires tracking state, applying rules consistently, or producing proofs. this essay examines those failure modes carefully, what they reveal about the architecture, and what open problems they suggest for students interested in ai research.
